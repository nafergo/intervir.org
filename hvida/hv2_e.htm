Living Memoirs...

Manuela Gonçalves  

 

     I have many memoirs from my thirty years teaching experience. Memoirs which invade myself in several, more or less opportune moments, but most frequently in teaching situations, in front of students - young future teachers - whose motivation to teach is too light (I shall do a small investigation work on this subject …) before the challenges they will have to face.

     It was at one of those moments, speaking about professional development, about what is to be a teacher nowadays, an exhausting and pleasant profession, about joy and tedium, but mainly about the lack of routine, when I was struck by one of those remembrances.

     It was 18:30 in a warm nightfall of a distant month of April … At Praça Marquês de Pombal, in Lisbon, thousands of teachers, coming from all over the country, carrying demanding, amusing, colourful, creative posters, identifying  many towns and villages from the inland and coastal areas, from mountains and plains, marching in a demonstration, hand in hand, singing and shouting slogans in one voice … They were thousands, different but together… They might not completely share the same points of view; they could even disagree on the measures they were demanding there, but they were together; they might not share the same ideas about education, as a public service, but they were together there, feeling as a part of a wider professional group ¾ Teachers. Being together they felt they had a voice and produced professional identity … Profession is also produced in critical moments.

    Marching with thousands of teachers, where the feminine was predominant, (another theme we can reflect about) exchanging words and opinions, singing and shouting slogans, has made more to build a professional conscience than many days working in a ‘escola preparatória’, about 40 km far  from the capital, in a town I’ll call Land of Lost Dreams. If I left there many of my dreams, it was also there I saw coming up and grow many of other dreams I nourished and, in spite of some disappointment, I am still sustaining. It was a school like many others, faced with the monotonous everyday life of resigned teachers , allways recalling their old times, in which “students respected the teachers”, “showed interest” and all those clichés we find in every teachers’ room …Of course, the ministry of education was always the only one to be blamed for the situation, those “who are in charge” but are not “here in the school with them….”.   I have never quite understood if those statements meant real nostalgia for the old time or if they were just mere words to justify the routine in which their daily work was plunged.

     There, among those thousands of teachers who I had never met, I felt alive, strong enough to overcome conformism and above all the indifference and public service environment prevailing  in the school I had left two hours ago. I also felt a bit ashamed not to be able often to face that conformism “not to raise problems” in a corporative attitude which I rejected on principle, but which I not always could overcome.

     My eyes wandered over the crow, rested on determined faces and on the coloured letters of the posters raised by hands used to handle chalk and books, I searched for strength and energy in the slow but resolute steps beside. John, a colleague from other school in the same town, companion in these walks, looked at me and smiled…

-         “It’s good to see so many people together, isn’t it? Maybe they are as isolated in their schools as we are in ours…”

-         “You’re right, and if a group like this would join in the same school?”

 Smiling, we plunged in the dream of what we could achieve but we kept walking along.

 Suddenly, as we turned into Braamcamp Str. - S. Bento (the Parliament) was the destination! -, a voice stood out over the slogans…

-         “Teacher Manuela! Teacher Manuela!”

-         “It’s for you,…” said John.

Surprised and somewhat unbelieving, I looked around. A crowded mixture of female faces ¾ young, less young, beautiful, decided …

-         “I don’t believe…I can’t see anyone I know…”

-         “It’s for you…look, it’s that group…there…on the right…they come from north…”

I looked at the group. About a dozen teachers, raising a poster where we could read “Bragança, Present!” was walking on that direction. They were rather young, full of life, smiling, and among them a female teacher was waving in my direction.

-         “Look, she must be wrong, I know none from Bragança!”  I replied to John, waving to the group. Probably she was mistaking me for someone else. After all, a crowd dissolves the individuality! It must be that. I kept walking but soon I noticed a young colleague coming to me, as the group shifted slightly to our side, presumably not to keep much apart.

-         “Excuse me, your name is Manuela, isn’t it?”

-         “Yes, it is. But do I know you?”

-         “Yes, you do…”

-         “Are you sure?”

-         “Yes, of course I am…”

-         “Of course?”

-         “Yes, and I’m here because of you.”

-         “Because of me?”

-         “Yes.”

-         “I don’t understand! What did I do?”

-         “You made me a teacher”.

I looked at her astonished and said nothing.

-         “You were my English teacher in Land of Lost Dreams”. She kept going, smiling…

-         “It was with you, in your classes, that I decided to be an English teacher… just like you. And now I am in Bragança, alone, but as an English teacher”.

Surprised and unbelieving, I asked:

-         “What is your name?”

-         “Isabel Rios”.

-         “I’m sorry Isabel, I don’t know what to say…”

-         Say nothing. After all I got what I wanted:  to be an English teacher. You aren’t to blame for what’s happening in the schools…” And she smiled sweetly, peacefully, just like I imagine the smile of satisfied teacher.

The demonstrators hastened their steps. S. Bento was getting closer and both John and the other Isabel’s colleagues called for our names. We could not lose from our friends. Isabel still said: -“You can’t imagine how happy I am to find you. You are still the same teacher I knew”. And she moved away.

Unable to say a single word, I lost her in the crowd.

I haven’t seen her again in more than twenty years. I felt stunned, happy and I was filled by an indescribable feeling, a mixture of pride and of certain anguish.  I kept silent and John, beside me, smiled.

-         “After all, we manage to change something, don’t you think?”

      I can’t exactly recall what happened next. I don’t remember who spoke on the assembly stairway, or which  demands we were fighting for and if they were won by all the teachers there. But I had already won. I, John, Isabel and eventually some of her colleagues with whom she might have commented on our reunion, all of us had won a new professional dimension. The certainty that it was I,  it was my work and my conviction the model for the young teacher who did not keep silent and resigned in her school.  She was there, proud of the profession she desired, though disappointed with some measures taken in the educational field.

     In spite of the school routine, which was slowly wearing me out and making my dreams sparse, I managed to “enchant” someone. Something had changed after all! John was right. We cannot give up and do nothing helpless before dominant conformism and apathy.

     Coming back home was like a return to   a dream, to the will of transforming school, to the persistent work of making dreams come true, and more experiences and more citizens as well. It was above all a return to a place of hope! The disappointment of the departure has been replaced by the delight of the return.

     I was possessed by a whirlwind of emotions. John and I spoke and spoke words, emotions, and projects. A consciousness emerged from our professional route: the real result of our work is only visible much later, even years later, and I was seized by a huge joy.

      Today, when reviewing this episode, I produce profession again. I may not know the result of my work, but I know that I am sowing something in the minds of the students who I occasionally meet along this road. Who knows if that light motivation today foreseen in my young students, future teachers, is not getting stronger, class after class, day after day, like a seed sprouting and growing… For that reason only, it is worth to go on and struggle against the disenchantment that keeps peeping here and there. Who knows how many “Isabel Rios” can I still motivate! Maybe, one day, I’ll come to know that!
